Executive Thesis

Earth intelligence is structurally expanding

Three macro forces reinforcing each other — AI cost reduction, geopolitical demand pull, and infrastructure monitoring pressure

The Earth intelligence market is not in a cyclical expansion — it is undergoing a structural shift in how geospatial signals are consumed. Three independent forces are accelerating simultaneously and reinforcing each other: AI is collapsing the cost and latency of converting raw geospatial data into decisions, geopolitics is raising the value of persistent sensing and sovereign supply chains, and the physical infrastructure transition is multiplying the monitoring workload on assets that were not built for today's operational environment.

Gartner forecasts that 80% of enterprise software will be multimodal by 2030 (up from <10% in 2024) — a direct enabler for operationalising imagery, SAR, and spatial signals at scale. In parallel, Gartner defines "Earth intelligence" as AI applied to EO data and forecasts direct vendor revenues rising to over $4.2bn by 2030, with enterprises becoming the majority buyer.

Total Addressable Market — Layer A

Global geospatial intelligence market baselines

Multiple market definitions presented transparently — conservative core GI baseline chosen for investor diligence

The market is reported with materially different scopes depending on whether consumer location-based services, mapping, and broad GIS software are included. The correct approach for investor diligence is to present multiple baselines, then choose a conservative "core GI" baseline aligned with Involve Space's revenue model — enterprise and government infrastructure, national security, and regulated sectors rather than consumer LBS.

These serve as external reasonableness checks on the GeoAI baseline without double-counting.

Serviceable Available Market

SAM — Europe + NATO + US, infrastructure and defence verticals

Geography and vertical filters applied to TAM to reach the addressable market for Involve Space's current positioning

SAM is defined as Europe + NATO-aligned markets + United States, constrained to verticals where (i) sovereignty/dual-use procurement matters and (ii) persistent monitoring creates quantifiable ROI: critical infrastructure monitoring (including energy), defence/national security, and P&C insurance applications for asset and catastrophe risk.

Geographic filter — 60–70% regional share
North America + Europe = 66% of EO revenue
Two independent data points support a 60–70% "sovereign-aligned demand share" assumption:

EO revenue concentration (Novaspace/Reuters): North America = 44%, Europe = 22% → combined 66% of global EO revenue in 2023.

Defence spend (SIPRI): NATO members represent 55% of global military expenditure in 2024 ($1.506T of $2.718T).

SAM applies a 0.60–0.70 regional accessibility multiplier to the relevant vertical slice of TAM.
Vertical filter — 30–45% of GeoAI market
Infrastructure, defence, and regulated sectors only
Involve Space excludes GI demand driven by consumer and advertising use cases. There is no single universally accepted published split of the GeoAI market by vertical — this memo therefore uses an explicit conservative assumption:

Vertical relevance factor (assumption): 30–45% of the global GeoAI/geospatial intelligence market corresponds to infrastructure/energy, defence/national security, and insurance/financial risk use cases where sovereign or regulated procurement is a meaningful factor.

This is consistent with the prominence of defence and infrastructure in EO demand taxonomies, and Gartner's framing of Earth intelligence use cases.

SOM is modelled from capacity constraints (fleet scale and deployments) rather than market share assumptions

SOM is bounded by (i) platform count, (ii) operational uptime, (iii) sensor mix and re-tasking, and (iv) geographic/regulatory constraints. This approach is appropriate for persistent stratospheric infrastructure: even with strong demand, revenue is limited by fleet capacity — not by market share dynamics. This is a conservative and auditable framing.

Competitive Landscape

Where Involve Space sits structurally

Integrated sensing + autonomous control + GeoAI platform — distinct from pure satellite operators, pure analytics vendors, and defence integrators

Involve Space sits in a hybrid category that does not map cleanly onto existing market participants. Pure satellite operators provide data but cannot offer persistence or sovereign data custody. Pure analytics vendors can produce insights but depend on third-party data streams they cannot control or guarantee. Defence primes and integrators orchestrate systems but do not own novel sovereign sensing infrastructure. Involve is the only active European company attempting to own the full stack from stratospheric collection to sovereign intelligence delivery.

Investor-grade conclusion: The market is large enough on a conservative basis ($37bn → $63bn by 2030 for GeoAI GI) and the vertical demand substrates are strong (energy grid expansion and defence spend concentration). The primary diligence question is therefore not "does demand exist?" but "can Involve Space scale the fleet, secure sovereign-aligned contracts, and convert persistent sensing into repeatable workflows faster than pure-play data providers and integrators can commoditise the analytics layer?" The SOM model shows that, if fleet scale is achieved, revenue can become material by 2028 without requiring implausible market share assumptions — but the gating factors remain procurement cycles, regulatory constraints, and operational execution of persistent stratospheric deployments.
