Overview

What the Neurostar engine is

A vertically integrated simulation and autonomy platform -- built entirely in-house

Neurostar is Involve Space's proprietary software engine for simulating, designing, and autonomously navigating stratospheric balloon platforms. It is not a collection of off-the-shelf tools configured together -- every subsystem, from the physics model to the AI navigation agent, was built from scratch by the internal team, giving the company full control over accuracy, speed, and the ability to iterate rapidly.

The engine covers the entire mission lifecycle: it starts from a blank set of mission requirements, produces a physical balloon design, simulates in detail how that balloon will behave in a real stratospheric environment, and then trains an AI agent capable of controlling altitude autonomously in the conditions those simulations revealed. The same data model flows uninterrupted through all four stages -- design, simulation, environment, and AI -- guaranteeing physical consistency end-to-end.

Why build it in-house? Commercial flight simulation tools are not designed for stratospheric balloons. They cannot model the coupled interaction between gas mechanics, film thermodynamics, wind-layer navigation, and an AI control agent simultaneously. Building Neurostar as a single integrated codebase means every parameter touched during design is traceable through simulation all the way to the AI's decision-making logic.
System Architecture

Four vertically integrated layers

Each layer feeds the next -- the output of one becomes the input of the one below

Figure 1 — The four vertically integrated layers of Neurostar. A single shared design file flows from Layer 1 through each subsequent stage, ensuring physical consistency across the full stack.

The key architectural decision is that these four layers are not loosely coupled services -- they are tightly integrated components sharing a single data model. When a new balloon design is created in Layer 1, it immediately propagates into the physics model, the environment simulator, and the observation space the AI agent operates in. There is no translation layer, no format mismatch, and no opportunity for inconsistency to creep in.

Layer 1 -- Design

The Balloon Designer

From mission parameters to a physics-ready specification in seconds

Before any simulation can run, the balloon must be designed. The Balloon Designer takes a set of mission-level requirements -- target altitude, payload mass, how long the balloon needs to stay aloft -- and produces a complete physical specification: the exact volume and shape of the envelope, the right amount of lifting gas, the mass of the film, the battery capacity, and whether solar panels are needed. This process is fully parametric and automated.

Three distinct design procedures cover the three platform types Involve Space operates. Each procedure includes physical constraint checking -- the designer will not produce a design that would cause the film to tear, the balloon to fly too high, or the energy budget to run out before the mission ends.

Figure 2 — The Balloon Designer processes inputs into a detailed design file.
⚖️
Buoyancy equilibrium
The core design constraint is simple: the lifting force of the gas must exactly balance the total weight of the system at the target altitude. The designer solves this balance simultaneously with the geometric constraint that the balloon must physically contain enough gas to reach that altitude.
💪
Material stress limits
The polyethylene film has a maximum stress it can bear before tearing. The designer checks that the pressure differential at flight altitude never exceeds this limit, using the real non-linear mechanical behaviour of the specific film used in production.
⚡
Energy budget closure
For active platforms, the designer calculates the solar power available at stratospheric altitude and verifies that the battery can sustain compressor operations through night periods, sizing both components for the specified mission duration.
🎯
Altitude control range
The double-balloon architecture requires the designer to calculate the ballonnet volume range -- the amount of air that can be pumped in or vented out -- to guarantee the balloon can actively traverse the full desired altitude band during operations.
Layer 2 -- Physics

The Physics Engine

A high-fidelity digital twin of a stratospheric balloon in flight

The physics engine is the beating heart of Neurostar. It maintains a complete digital twin of a balloon in flight -- tracking dozens of physical quantities simultaneously and advancing the simulation forward in time step by step. Every decision the AI agent makes is tested against this model before it is ever deployed on a real platform.

Four tightly coupled subsystems contribute to the physics computation at every timestep. They interact continuously: the thermal behaviour of the gas affects buoyancy, which affects altitude, which changes the atmospheric pressure, which changes the thermal exchange -- and so on. No subsystem can be evaluated in isolation.

Figure 3 — Physics engine computation loop. All subsystems interact at each step to produce a physically consistent state update, which becomes the input for the next timestep.
Balloon Dynamics
Core physics module
Primary
Integrates the equations of motion of the balloon through time: buoyancy, weight, drag, and the mechanical behaviour of the gas envelope. Models how pressure, volume, and temperature inside the gas chamber and the air ballonnet relate to each other -- and how that relationship changes as the balloon traverses layers of different air density. The altitude control logic flows directly from this model: pumping air in makes the balloon heavier and denser relative to the surrounding air, causing it to sink; venting air has the opposite effect.
Thermal Exchange
Heat balance
Critical for altitude
Stratospheric balloon altitude is strongly influenced by gas temperature. This module tracks all heat flows simultaneously: sunlight absorbed through the film, infrared radiation from Earth below and the cold sky above, convective heat transfer with the surrounding air, and the internal exchange between the film and the lifting gas. When the sun heats the gas during the day, the balloon naturally rises; as it cools overnight, it sinks. Understanding and anticipating this thermal cycle is essential for maintaining station-keeping.
Solar Position
Astronomical model
Day/night critical
Calculates precisely where the sun sits in the sky at any given date, time, and geographic location, and therefore how much solar radiation reaches the balloon's film and panels. This directly drives both the thermal model -- determining how much the gas heats up -- and the energy model -- determining how much electricity the solar array generates. The model accounts for the thinner atmosphere at stratospheric altitude, which transmits more solar radiation than at ground level.
Onboard Devices
Power and actuation
4 components
Models every energy-relevant component as a physically realistic unit rather than a simplified abstraction. The solar array accounts for each panel's orientation relative to the sun moment by moment. The battery tracks its state of charge and prevents the compressor from operating if energy runs out. The compressor replicates the real performance characteristics of the actual centrifugal compressor used on the gondola. The vent valve models how pressurised air escapes under different conditions, including two distinct flow regimes depending on the pressure differential.
Layer 3 -- Environment

The Atmospheric Engine

Two interchangeable wind models -- one for accuracy, one for training speed

The atmosphere is what makes stratospheric navigation uniquely complex. Wind direction and speed vary dramatically with altitude -- sometimes reversing completely within a few hundred metres -- and forecast accuracy degrades with time. Neurostar includes two atmospheric backends that share a common interface and can be swapped without changing any other part of the system.

Figure 4 — Two atmospheric backends sharing a common interface. The real-world mode uses actual forecast data; the training mode generates synthetic but physically plausible wind fields at high speed.

The synthetic wind generator is particularly important for AI training. A real AI agent needs to experience thousands of different wind scenarios to generalise well -- it must not overfit to a small set of historical weather patterns. The generator creates wind fields that are internally physically coherent but never exactly repeated between training episodes. It layers four separate components: a slow-evolving background wind that drifts in direction and speed, two to four distinct wind shear layers at different altitudes (replicating the real stratospheric structure where adjacent layers often blow in opposite directions), spatial turbulence, and a weak vertical component. Together they produce a rich, non-repetitive environment that forces the AI agent to learn genuine navigation principles rather than memorise specific scenarios.

Strategic Concept

The Art of Stratospheric Sailing

Horizontal autonomy through vertical control

To understand why Neurostar requires artificial intelligence, one must first understand the fundamental strategic puzzle of the stratosphere. Unlike an airplane or a drone, a stratospheric balloon has no propellers or jets to push it forward. It is, in the simplest terms, a sailing vessel that uses the atmosphere's vertical structure as its medium.

The stratosphere is not a uniform mass of air. It is a complex, layered highway system. At any given moment, the wind at 18 km might be blowing East at 20 knots, while at 21 km it is blowing South-West, and at 24 km it might be nearly still. This vertical diversity is the key to navigation: by changing its altitude, the balloon can "choose" which wind layer to catch.

Vision — Vertical control is the "steering wheel" of a stratospheric platform. By moving up or down, the engine chooses the most efficient horizontal trajectory.

This is Three-Dimensional Chess played at 100,000 feet. A mission to stay over a specific city (station-keeping) or to reach a remote location involves calculating exactly when to rise and when to sink to exploit these shifting flows. It is a problem of optimization: you must balance reaching your destination with conserving energy, managing gas temperatures, and predicting how the forecast will change.

This is why the AI exists. A human pilot cannot mentally process the non-linear physics of the balloon, the battery's energy curve, and the global wind forecast simultaneously for 60 days straight. The AI Navigation Agent does exactly this: it transforms the complex, chaotic reality of the stratosphere into a predictable, autonomous path.

Layer 4 -- AI Navigation

The Autonomous Navigation Agent

A deep reinforcement learning system that discovers stratospheric navigation by experience

The navigation agent is the component that transforms Neurostar from a sophisticated simulation tool into a genuine autonomous control system. The fundamental idea behind it is simple to state but profound in its implications: rather than being programmed with a fixed set of rules that a human engineer has reasoned out in advance, the agent learns entirely through experience. It is placed inside the simulated environment, it tries things, it sees what happens, and over time it develops an increasingly refined understanding of how to keep the balloon where it needs to be.

To understand why this matters, it helps to consider what the alternative looks like. A classical control system for a stratospheric balloon would consist of a set of hand-crafted rules: if the balloon is too low, turn on the compressor for a certain number of seconds; if it is too high, open the valve. In principle, this works. In practice, it encounters an immediate problem -- the balloon is never in a static, predictable state. The lifting gas is continuously heating and cooling as day turns to night. The wind at the balloon's current altitude may be pushing it toward a region where the atmosphere at other altitudes behaves completely differently. The battery may be running low, limiting how aggressive any correction can be. A rule that says "if too low, compress" does not know, and cannot know, that compressing now will deplete the battery just before a long night period during which the gas will cool and naturally cause the balloon to descend further. An agent that has internalised the dynamics of the system as a whole can make decisions that anticipate these interactions, rather than reacting to each variable in isolation.

Training the agent works through a cycle of observation, action, and feedback that repeats continuously across hundreds of simulated mission episodes. At each step, the agent receives a snapshot of the balloon's current state -- its altitude relative to the target, how fast it is climbing or descending, how much energy is left in the battery, how full the gas chamber and the ballonnet are, and what the wind is doing at its current position. From this snapshot, it chooses one of three possible commands: engage the compressor to pump air into the ballonnet and push the balloon downward, open the valve to release air and allow the balloon to rise, or do nothing and let the system equilibrate naturally. The physics engine then executes the command and advances the simulation forward in time. The agent receives a signal indicating whether the outcome was good or bad: positive if the balloon moved closer to its target altitude, negative if it drifted further away, and a large penalty if it crossed a critical limit such as battery depletion or altitude loss.

This cycle -- observe, decide, act, receive feedback -- is the complete training loop. It contains no instructions about how to fly. The agent is never told to anticipate thermal cycles, or to conserve energy before nightfall, or to exploit wind shear layers for lateral positioning. These behaviours emerge naturally from the accumulated experience of thousands of training episodes, each one with a different wind pattern, a different time of day, a different starting condition. What appears to an outside observer as intelligent navigation strategy is, at its root, a very large number of these experiences compressed into the weights of a neural network.

Figure 5 — Detailed Reinforcement Learning training loop structure.

One of the subtler challenges in this kind of training is the long-horizon nature of the decisions. Opening the valve right now might look like a sensible correction because the balloon is slightly above target -- but if the sun is about to set and the gas temperature will drop naturally in the next twenty minutes, the balloon would have descended on its own without any intervention. The agent has wasted a portion of its ballonnet air capacity unnecessarily, and that air may be needed later. Conversely, waiting too long to intervene when the balloon is rising rapidly can let it drift far outside the acceptable altitude band before a correction becomes effective. The AI must learn to reason about the future trajectory of the system, not just its current state. This capacity for anticipatory, temporally-extended reasoning is precisely what makes deep reinforcement learning a qualitatively different tool from classical controllers -- and what requires so many training episodes to develop well.

Once the training process is complete, the learned policy is not something that needs to be adapted or translated for the physical world. Every input the agent used during simulation corresponds directly to a measurement available from sensors on the real gondola. The altitude error comes from GPS and barometric pressure. The battery state comes from the power management system. The wind reading comes from on-board air speed sensors. When the trained network is uploaded to the on-board computer, it begins operating in exactly the same way it did in simulation -- because the simulation was built to match the real world precisely. There is no gap between the learned behaviour and the deployed behaviour.

Distributional Reinforcement Learning
Core algorithm -- QDQN
Key differentiator
Most AI control systems learn to estimate the expected outcome of each possible action -- essentially, an average of what tends to happen when that action is taken. This is a reasonable starting point, but it hides important information. Two actions might have the same average outcome but very different distributions around that average: one might be reliable and predictable, while the other occasionally produces excellent results but sometimes leads to a dangerous state. An agent that only knows the average cannot distinguish between them.

Neurostar's navigation agent uses a more sophisticated approach: it learns the full distribution of possible outcomes for each action, not just the average. This means it always knows not only what is likely to happen, but also how uncertain the outcome is. In a stratospheric environment -- where the same command can have meaningfully different consequences depending on subtle atmospheric conditions that no sensor fully captures -- this richer understanding translates directly into better decisions. When outcomes are uncertain, the agent can choose to be more conservative. When the situation is clear, it can act decisively. The ability to calibrate its own confidence, rather than always committing to the action with the highest average expectation, is a critical safety property for a system operating autonomously at 25 km altitude with no possibility of immediate human intervention.
MarcoPolo Training Strategy
Proprietary exploration method
Proprietary approach
Every reinforcement learning system faces a fundamental tension: the agent tends to gravitate toward strategies it already knows work, because those strategies generate reliable positive rewards. But this means it may never discover better strategies that require passing through a period of uncertain or even negative outcomes first. It also means it may never encounter the rare but consequential situations -- the extreme wind reversals, the near-depleted battery, the borderline structural pressure -- that it would need to handle well on a real flight.

The MarcoPolo strategy -- named for the spirit of deliberate exploration into unknown territory -- addresses this at the level of entire training episodes rather than individual actions. A deliberately chosen fraction of training episodes are conducted with entirely random commands, with no learned policy involved at all. This guarantees regular exposure to edge cases the agent's current knowledge would lead it to avoid. The rest of the episodes use the evolving learned strategy, with exploration gradually giving way to exploitation as the policy matures. The two modes work in a productive cycle: pure exploration discovers new territory and new situations, and policy training maps those discoveries into lasting behavioural improvements. The result is a trained agent that has seen not just the most common scenarios but the full range of conditions a real flight might encounter -- including the ones it would be hardest to recover from if encountered for the first time in the air.
Safety Filter
Operational safety layer
Always active
Even the most carefully trained AI system operates within the boundaries of what it has experienced during training. A well-designed training process maximises the breadth of those experiences, but it cannot guarantee that every conceivable edge case has been covered. In some of those uncovered situations, the agent might produce a command that is physically inconsistent with the balloon's actual state -- commanding the valve to release air when the ballonnet is already empty, for instance, or attempting to run the compressor when the battery has insufficient energy for even a brief actuation.

The safety filter exists to ensure that no matter what the AI agent proposes, the actual command that reaches the balloon's actuators can never violate a physical constraint. It works by intercepting every command before execution, checking it against the real state of the system, and substituting the safest valid alternative if the proposed command would cause a problem. The agent itself is never aware that this intervention has occurred -- it simply receives a state update that reflects the physical world. The architecture ensures that even in genuine edge cases, the worst the AI can produce is a suboptimal manoeuvre, not a dangerous one.

Critically, the safety filter is active during simulation training and during real-world flight. The agent learns to navigate within the same constraints that govern its deployed behaviour -- which means the safety boundaries are never experienced as a surprise in the field.
From simulation to flight. The policy the agent develops during training is not specific to the simulator. Every input it uses during training -- altitude error, climb rate, battery charge, chamber fill levels, wind reading -- corresponds directly to a sensor available on the physical gondola. When the training is complete, the navigation policy transfers to the on-board flight computer without modification, converting months of simulated experience into real-world autonomous capability.
Design Philosophy

Engineering principles behind the platform

The architectural decisions that give Neurostar a structural advantage

🔗
One shared data model
Every layer of the system reads from a single shared design file. When a parameter changes, the change propagates automatically through all downstream layers. There is no risk of the AI being trained on a different balloon than the one being designed and tested.
🧪
Physics over approximation
Every subsystem uses real physical models derived from first principles or experimental measurements. Approximations are only used where they have been validated against the full physical model, and their accuracy range is explicitly documented.
⚡
Two-speed operation
For AI training, speed is critical. The atmospheric engine provides a fast synthetic mode that generates realistic wind fields without any database access. For mission planning and validation, the same engine switches to real forecast data. The physics model maintains the same fidelity in both modes.
🛡️
Safety by design
Safety constraints are embedded at the lowest level of the architecture. The safety filter acts before any command reaches the physics model. Material limits are checked inside the designer before any design can be saved. The AI reward function makes dangerous states intrinsically unattractive to a well-trained agent.
📡
Portable to real flights
The AI policy trained in simulation deploys directly on-board the gondola without modification. Every input the agent uses in simulation corresponds exactly to a sensor available on the physical platform. No input is synthetic or simulator-specific.
🔄
Designed to generalise
The MarcoPolo strategy and synthetic atmospheric generator together ensure the AI agent encounters an extremely wide range of scenarios during training, not just the most common ones. This breadth is what gives the agent robustness to unexpected atmospheric conditions or equipment states during a real mission.
Planning Ecosystem

Mission Planning Tools

A comprehensive suite to plan and predict stratospheric missions before launch

The planning environment takes initial configurations and environmental data, feeding them through specialized tools to produce an overall reliable trajectory and landing prediction.

Figure 7 — Planning Tools overview, translating initial parameters and environmental data into final expected trajectories.
StratoRelay
Deterministic Trajectory
Planning core
The primary tool for mission pathfinding. It combines high-fidelity physics with real GFS wind forecasts to predict the exactly trajectory of a specific balloon design from launch to landing. It accounts for diurnal thermal cycles and battery constraints to ensure mission feasibility.
StratoEye
Reachability Analysis
Stochastic Intelligence
A powerful diagnostic tool that computes the Reachability Heatmap (CAT Map) for any point on Earth. Using Value Iteration and dynamic programming, StratoEye solves the Markov Decision Process of navigation to determine the probability of a balloon remaining within range of a target over a multi-day horizon.

Key Innovation: Features a persistent 0.25° x 0.25° grid-based caching system that stores verified navigation states. This allows the engine to resume computations from previous runs, significantly reducing analysis time for complex scenarios and enabling a "time-scrubbable" visualization of reachability evolution over 15+ day periods.
StratoStats
Probabilistic Landing
Risk assessment
Runs Monte Carlo simulations by perturbing wind data and physics parameters to generate a "landing footprint." This statistical approach identifies the most likely recovery zones and quantifies the risk of landing in restricted or hazardous areas.
StratoLoop
Long-duration analysis
Station-keeping
Specialized for month-long station-keeping missions. It analyzes the recurrence of wind patterns to identify "stable orbits" in the stratosphere where a balloon can remain over a geographic area of interest for extended durations with minimal actuation.

Supporting Modules

Three supporting subsystems

Specialised components that underpin accuracy and deployability across the core layers

Film Material Model
Structural mechanics
Design and simulation
Models the real mechanical behaviour of the polyethylene film used in balloon manufacturing. The key complexity is that this film does not deform in a simple linear way -- its resistance to stretching changes significantly across different elongation levels, meaning a standard linear model would mispredict both the design safety margin and the balloon's dynamic response during flight. The material module captures this non-linear behaviour accurately, allowing the designer to guarantee structural integrity and the physics engine to predict envelope deformation correctly under changing pressure conditions.
Wind Prediction Learner
Adaptive correction
Online adaptation
Atmospheric forecast models are never perfectly accurate -- there is always a difference between the predicted wind and the actual wind at any given altitude and time. This module uses a statistical learning technique (Gaussian Process regression) to build a model of these forecast errors as the balloon gathers real observations, and apply corrections in real time to improve trajectory predictions for nearby altitudes and future timesteps. The correction improves progressively throughout a mission as more observations accumulate.
Coordinate Manager
Navigation geometry
Always active
GPS coordinates are intuitive for humans but inconvenient for physics calculations, which work best in a flat Cartesian system where distances are measured in metres. This module transparently converts between the two representations -- allowing the physics engine to work in metres while mission planning, display, and output reporting use standard geographic coordinates. The projection is anchored to the mission launch point to keep distortion negligible across the typical mission footprint of a few hundred kilometres.
